The Lectures on Faith

     What are they?  Who wrote them, and when?  A surprisingly large number of the restoration believers have never heard of them, so naturally are ignorant of what they contain. 

     These 7 lectures were delivered by Joseph Smith at the School of the Prophets in Kirtland, Ohio about 1833,  prior to sending the elders forth to the world on their various missions.  They were given for the purpose of helping the elders gain a better understanding of the doctrine they were to teach unto the world.  The committee of four formed  in September, 1831, to compile the book later known as the Doctrine and Covenants of the Church of Jesus Christ, during their 4 year stand,  agreed to put these lectures in the forepart of the book.  In the General Assembly of the Church of August 1835, all the quorums present voted to accept the book as compiled by the committee and using the records of that meeting the following statement:  "Elder John Smith, taking the lead of the High Council in Kirtland, bore record that the revelations in said book were true, and that the lectures were judiciously arranged and compiled, and were profitable for doctrine; whereupon the High Council of Kirtland accepted them and acknowledged them as the Doctrine and Covenants of their faith, by a unanimous vote".   This was followed by unanimous acceptance of the other contents by the Assembly.   The edition of the 1835 was printed thus;  with the Lectures on Faith in the front of the book, followed by the revelations given up to that time, and the other material in it's place.  There the Lectures remained in all subsequent editions of the book up to 1897 when one of the larger factions voted to omit them.  In 1921 another large faction followed suit and discontinued printing them in the book.   Today both factions describe them as being of historical value only.  When we read and study these lectures we can begin to understand why they were omitted and no longer printed.  They teach a road that is far too close for anyone but those with the Spirit of God and a desire to learn.  As Brother (Rupert) Fletcher in his book "Alpheus Cutler and the Church of Jesus Christ"   page 332 asked, "How much have the Churches gained by taking out the Lectures?   Has it proven an advantage, and has it brought about a closed unity among the members?"  This is a very good question;  how would you answer it?   The people who are trying to serve their Lord need all the help they can get to combat the power of evil; and do we gain the strength necessary when we toss this help away?  Lecture (number) 6, especially, contains instruction and statements that any man seeking to gain his salvation cannot afford to deny or refuse to accept.  It calls upon man to lay down his all, his character and reputation, his honor and applause, his good name among men, his houses, his lands, his brothers and sisters, his wife and children, and even his own life also;  counting all things but filth and dross, for the excellency of the knowledge of Jesus Christ.  Now can you begin to understand why these lectures are not popular with those having the natural mind of man?  And can you not begin to realize the seriousness of the cause that the principles taught in these lectures seek to build up?  We can begin to understand why the original church officials voted they were profitable for the doctrine and wanted them included in that book.  Man at best does a pretty sorry job of serving their God, and with the obstacles placed in their path by the powers of evil, only by the acceptance of the truths contained in these lectures can this be thwarted.  What spirit would you say actuated those leaders in these two factions when they voted the lectures were no longer of value as doctrine and omitted them from that book?  Who was right, the early officers who voted they should be in there and were profitable for doctrine, or those who voted later that they were not?  As Brother Fletcher asked, " did they profit from it?"   To my way of thinking, they did their members a great disservice by depriving all those following their leadership, of the truths contained in those lectures.  Very few people, belonging to these factions of the present generation, have even hear of the lectures, and of those who have, very few are acquainted with their contents, and yet the early officers of the Church counted them of great value.  Would you say that was advancement?
